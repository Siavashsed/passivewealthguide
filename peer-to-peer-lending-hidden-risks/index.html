Peer-to-Peer Lending: What the Platforms Don't Tell You Before You Sign Up

A retired CFP reveals the hidden risks of peer-to-peer lending that platforms bury in fine print - and how to protect yourself.

The pitch is genuinely appealing. You loan money directly to borrowers, skip the bank entirely, and collect interest rates somewhere between 6% and 12% annually. The platforms make it look clean, almost passive. And for a while, when I first tested P2P lending back in 2017 across two platforms with a modest $8,000 allocation, it did feel that way. The returns showed up. The dashboard looked healthy. Everything was fine, right up until it wasn't.

The Default Rate Problem Nobody Advertises Clearly

Every P2P platform publishes an "expected return" figure. What they're far quieter about is how that number is calculated, and whether it accounts for the loans that go completely silent. According to a 2019 study by the Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance, default rates on consumer P2P loans in the UK averaged around 3.1% annually during a period of economic stability. That might sound manageable until you realize a 3% default rate on a 7% return already cuts your real yield to somewhere around 4%, and that's before platform fees and tax.

The tricky part is that defaults don't announce themselves on a schedule. A borrower misses a payment, then another, then the account enters a collections process that can drag on for 12 to 18 months. During that time, your money is locked. You're not earning on it, but you're also not getting it back. The platform might show you an "in recovery" label, which sounds optimistic. In practice, recovery rates on unsecured consumer loans hover around 40% in most cases, meaning you'll likely see less than half of what you're owed.

When I was working through my own P2P allocation during that testing period, two of my loans went into default within the first 14 months. Combined, they represented about $900 of my capital. I eventually recovered around $370. The platform's headline return figure for that year? Still showing 8.2%. The math only works if you read everything carefully and do your own accounting, which most people don't.

Liquidity Is a Feature They Sell and a Problem They Underplay

Most platforms offer what they call a "secondary market" so you can sell your loan notes before they mature and get your money out early. This sounds like genuine liquidity. And when conditions are calm and investor demand is strong, it can work reasonably well. But the secondary market is not a bank account. It's a marketplace, and marketplaces dry up when everyone wants to sell at the same time.

During the early months of 2020, several major P2P platforms froze withdrawals entirely or suspended their secondary markets. Investors who assumed they could access their capital within a few days found themselves waiting months. Some are still waiting. This isn't a fringe scenario, it's a predictable consequence of how the model works under stress. The platform can't conjure buyers from nothing if sentiment turns.

So if you're considering P2P lending as part of a passive income setup, treat that money as genuinely locked for the full loan term. Build your strategy around that assumption. If the secondary market works and you get out early, great. But if you're counting on that flexibility for anything time-sensitive, like covering a gap in living expenses or rebalancing your portfolio quickly, you're taking on more risk than the marketing suggests.

The Tax Treatment Will Surprise You at Filing Time

This is the one that catches people most off guard, and I've seen this pattern repeatedly in my work with clients who came to me after their first year in P2P lending. The interest you earn is taxable as ordinary income, not at the lower capital gains rate. That's already less favorable than dividends from stocks held long-term. But here's where it gets genuinely confusing: when a loan defaults and you lose principal, the tax treatment of that loss depends on your jurisdiction, your holding period, and sometimes the specific structure of the platform.

In the US, defaulted P2P loans are generally treated as bad debt deductions, which are classified as short-term capital losses. You can offset those against capital gains, but if your losses exceed your gains, you're limited to deducting $3,000 per year against ordinary income. If you've lost $9,000 across three defaults, you're carrying forward that deduction for three years while you already paid tax on the interest income that was supposed to offset the risk. The timing mismatch is genuinely painful.

My practical advice here is simple: before you put a dollar into any P2P platform, sit down with a tax professional who understands investment income. Don't rely on the platform's FAQ for this. Their tax guidance is general at best and self-serving at worst. The after-tax return on P2P lending is almost always lower than the headline figure implies, and for investors in higher tax brackets, the math can become quite unflattering quite fast.

How to Use P2P Lending Without Getting Burned

None of this means you should avoid peer-to-peer lending entirely. My actual position is that it can serve a real purpose for the right investor in the right proportion. But "right proportion" matters more than most people realize. I'd argue it shouldn't represent more than 5% to 8% of a passive income portfolio, particularly for anyone still building toward financial independence. It's a yield-enhancer, not a foundation.

Diversification within the platform matters too. Spreading across 200 or more individual loans, rather than concentrating in 10 or 20, smooths out the impact of individual defaults meaningfully. Some platforms automate this for you, which is genuinely useful. And sticking to the platform's higher-grade loan categories, typically labeled A or AA, will reduce your yield slightly but cut your default exposure significantly. The extra 2% return from lower-grade loans rarely compensates for the additional default risk in my experience.

And one more thing worth saying plainly: platform risk is real. The P2P company itself can fail. Several have. In the UK alone, Lendy collapsed in 2019 and left thousands of investors facing significant losses, with recovery processes still unresolved years later. Check whether the platform has a wind-down plan, how client funds are held, and whether there's any regulatory oversight. In the US, look for SEC registration. In the UK, FCA authorization. It's not glamorous due diligence, but it's the kind that protects you when things go sideways.

Here's the one thing you can do today: pull up the platform you're considering and look specifically for their historical default data, their secondary market suspension history, and their wind-down policy. Peer-to-peer lending can be a useful piece of a passive income strategy, but only if you go in with your eyes open rather than dazzled by the headline rate.
