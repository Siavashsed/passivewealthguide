REITs vs Rental Property: A Real Numbers Comparison (From Someone Who's Done Both)

Sarah Mills compares REITs vs rental property with real numbers, honest risks, and a clear verdict from a CFP who retired at 41.

By   Sarah Mills Filed   Passive Income & Investing Read   4 min

Most people frame this debate wrong. They ask which one is better, as if there's a universal answer waiting to be discovered. But when I was building the passive income portfolio that let me retire at 41, I held both REITs and a rental duplex at the same time, and the comparison was a lot messier than any blog post I'd read suggested. The duplex generated a higher gross yield. It also ate two weekends and a $6,400 HVAC replacement in year three. The REITs hummed along and deposited money into my brokerage account like clockwork.

The Yield Numbers Look Closer Than You'd Expect

A single-family rental in a mid-tier market might generate a gross yield of 7 to 9 percent on purchase price. That sounds appealing until you subtract vacancy (plan for 8 percent annually), property management if you want it to actually be passive (8 to 10 percent of rent), maintenance (budget 1 percent of property value per year), insurance, taxes, and occasional capital costs. Net cash-on-cash returns for residential rentals typically land somewhere between 4 and 6 percent in most markets today, and that's before accounting for your time.

Equity REITs, by comparison, have historically delivered total returns averaging around 9.5 percent annually over the past 25 years, according to data from NAREIT in 2023. That figure includes both dividend income and price appreciation. Current dividend yields on many established REITs sit between 3.5 and 5 percent, which is lower gross income than a rental on paper. But you're not replacing a roof. You're not fielding a call about a broken garbage disposal on a Tuesday night.

The comparison also shifts depending on how much capital you're deploying. A REIT investment starts at whatever a single share costs, sometimes under $20. A rental property in most functioning markets requires somewhere between $40,000 and $80,000 as a down payment, plus closing costs and reserves. That's a meaningful barrier, and it concentrates your risk in a single asset in a single zip code.

What the Tax Math Actually Looks Like

Tax treatment is where rental property genuinely shines, and I won't pretend otherwise. When I owned my duplex, depreciation write-offs sheltered a meaningful chunk of rental income from ordinary income tax each year. The IRS lets you depreciate residential property over 27.5 years, which creates a paper loss you can sometimes use to offset other income, depending on your income level and whether you qualify as an active participant or a real estate professional.

REITs pass through dividends that are largely classified as ordinary income, which gets taxed at your regular marginal rate. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act created a 20 percent deduction on qualified REIT dividends through the pass-through provision, which helps, but rental property depreciation can still outperform this in the right circumstances. If you're in the 32 or 37 percent bracket and actively managing property, the tax shelter from depreciation is real money.

But here's the nuance people miss: depreciation recapture hits when you sell. That accumulated depreciation comes back as taxable income at up to 25 percent. So the tax advantage is often a deferral, not a permanent escape. A 1031 exchange can kick the can further down the road, but only if you keep cycling into new properties. It's not a loophole most people can use indefinitely.

The Hidden Costs of 'Hands-Off' Rental Income

I've seen this pattern repeat with clients more times than I can count. Someone buys a rental property, hires a property manager, and expects it to behave like a dividend stock. It doesn't. Even with professional management, you're making decisions: approving repairs above a certain dollar threshold, reviewing lease renewals, handling insurance claims, and eventually dealing with capital improvements. The property doesn't disappear from your mental bandwidth just because someone else is doing the day-to-day work.

There's also liquidity risk that gets underestimated. If you need cash from a rental property, your options are sell (which takes months and costs 6 to 8 percent in transaction costs), refinance (which adds debt and has its own costs), or take out a HELOC. None of those are quick. With a REIT, you sell shares on a Tuesday and the cash settles in two business days. When I was structuring my retirement income in my late thirties, that liquidity difference genuinely mattered to my planning.

And concentration risk is real. One bad tenant, one major structural issue, one neighborhood that declines economically, and a single rental property can go from cash-flowing to cash-draining quickly. A diversified REIT holds hundreds or thousands of properties across sectors and geographies. You own a fraction of all of them. That's a fundamentally different risk profile, and arguably a safer one for someone without significant real estate experience.

Which One Actually Fits Your Life Right Now

The honest answer depends on three things: how much capital you have, how much time you're willing to trade, and what phase of wealth-building you're in. If you're earlier in your journey, have under $100,000 to invest, and want income that doesn't require active management, REITs are the cleaner choice. You can start immediately, diversify across property types (industrial, residential, healthcare, retail), and reinvest dividends automatically through a DRIP program without touching anything.

Rental property makes more sense when you have a larger capital base, want the tax benefits of depreciation, are comfortable with the operational realities, and ideally have some local market knowledge that gives you an edge on acquisition. In my experience, the investors who do best with rentals aren't passive at all in the early years. They're actively learning the market, managing relationships, and treating it like a part-time business. That's fine, but call it what it is.

One common misconception worth addressing directly: rental property is not automatically the better wealth builder just because it uses mortgage financing to control a larger asset. Yes, a $200,000 property bought with $50,000 down gives you exposure to appreciation on the full value. But it also gives you exposure to depreciation on the full value, and the mortgage payment doesn't pause if your tenant leaves. Borrowed money amplifies outcomes in both directions. So if you're choosing rentals primarily for that amplification effect, make sure you've stress-tested the scenario where the property sits vacant for four months.

Here's what I'd actually suggest doing today: pull up your current investment accounts and calculate what percentage of your portfolio is in real estate, either through REITs, property, or both. If it's close to zero, starting with a diversified REIT through a standard brokerage account costs you nothing but an afternoon of research and gives you immediate, liquid exposure to real estate income. If you already have capital set aside and you're genuinely interested in the operational side of property ownership, run the numbers on a specific deal in your local market using a realistic 5 percent vacancy rate, 10 percent management fee, and 1 percent annual maintenance budget. If it still cash-flows after all that, it might be worth pursuing. Both paths can build real wealth. The one that builds yours is the one you'll actually stick with.
