Passive Income With Digital Products: What Nobody Tells You About the First 18 Months

By Sarah Mills

Photo by Karina Finger / Pexels

Somewhere around 2019, I sold my first digital product. It was a 14-page budgeting workbook I'd put together for clients, converted to a PDF, and listed on Gumroad for $17. I made $51 in the first month. Three people I didn't know bought it. I remember thinking: this is either the beginning of something real, or a very slow way to make grocery money. It turned out to be both, depending on the month.

Here's my position, and I'll hold it through this entire article: digital products can absolutely generate meaningful passive income, but the timeline and the upfront effort required are almost always wildly underestimated by the people selling courses about selling courses. The math works. The fantasy version of it doesn't. So let me walk you through what actually tends to happen, what the numbers look like in practice, and how to decide whether this income stream fits your situation.

The Real Startup Curve Looks Nothing Like the Highlight Reels

Most digital product creators don't make significant money in month one, two, or even six. A 2022 study by Podia found that the median creator on their platform earned under $100 per month in their first year. Not per week. Per month. That number isn't discouraging if you go in knowing it, but it absolutely is discouraging if you've been told you can replace your salary in 90 days.

When I tested different price points and product types between 2019 and 2021, I noticed a consistent pattern: products that sold well had usually been promoted consistently for at least four to six months before they gained any real traction. The passive part comes later. The active part, the building, the audience-growing, the iterating, comes first and it takes longer than you think.

And the creators who quit early almost always quit right before the inflection point. I've seen this pattern repeatedly, both in my own experience and in conversations with clients who've tried this route. Month seven or eight is when things often start clicking, usually because by then you've collected enough real feedback to know what your audience actually wants to buy.

What Digital Products Actually Cost to Build (Time and Money)

People hear 'digital product' and think free to create. That's partially true and mostly misleading. A decent e-book or template might cost you nothing in software if you already own the tools, but it'll cost you somewhere between 20 and 80 hours of focused work to create something genuinely useful. An online course with recorded video? Multiply that by three or four, minimum.

There are also platform fees to factor in. Gumroad takes around 10% per transaction. Teachable and Kajabi run monthly fees that start around $39 and climb fast once you want real features. Payment processing adds another 2.9% plus 30 cents per transaction on most platforms. So if you're selling a $27 product, you're actually netting somewhere between $21 and $23 after fees, before taxes. That's not a dealbreaker, but it matters when you're projecting income.

The good news is that once the product exists and the systems are set up, your marginal cost per sale is genuinely close to zero. That's the actual appeal here, and it's legitimate. But don't confuse low ongoing cost with low initial cost. Both are real. They just happen at different points in time.

The Audience Problem Most Beginners Skip Right Past

Digital products don't sell themselves. I know that sounds obvious, but the marketing piece is where most first-timers badly underplan. Without an existing audience, whether that's an email list, a social following, or consistent search traffic to a website, you're essentially opening a shop in an empty field and hoping people wander by.

Building that audience is arguably the hardest part of the whole equation. It's also the part that takes the longest. In my experience, creators who already had even a modest email list of 500 to 1,000 engaged subscribers saw dramatically better launch results than those starting from zero. That list doesn't appear overnight. It typically takes six months to a year of consistent content to build it organically.

So if you're starting from scratch, I'd actually suggest treating the first year as an audience-building phase, not an income phase. If you're also building other income streams during that period, like dividend investing, that kind of parallel approach tends to relieve the pressure that causes people to give up. When Digital Products Finally Do Work, Here's What the Numbers Look Like

Let's talk honest projections. A well-positioned digital product priced between $27 and $97 sold to a warm audience of a few thousand followers might convert at 1% to 3% during a launch. That means if 2,000 people see your offer and 2% buy a $47 product, you've made $1,880 before fees. Do that twice a year with growing traffic, and you're looking at somewhere between $3,000 and $6,000 annually from a single product. That's not retirement money on its own, but it's real, and it compounds as your catalog and audience grow.

The creators I've seen consistently earn $2,000 to $5,000 per month from digital products almost always have multiple products, a steady content engine driving organic traffic, and an email list they've maintained for years. It's not passive in the set-it-and-forget-it sense. It's more like a business that increasingly runs with less of your daily attention over time. There's a meaningful difference between those two things.

For context, my own digital income became genuinely hands-off around the three-year mark. By then I had four products, a blog with consistent search traffic, and an email list of just over 4,000 people. The income varied month to month, but the floor was predictable enough to treat it like a real income stream. That consistency, combined with dividend income from a long-term portfolio, is what actually made early retirement possible. If you want to understand how the investment side of that equation worked, the piece on how dividend stocks build passive income over time explains the approach I trusted for the longer-term foundation.

Here's the one thing you can do today: write down the specific problem you could solve for a specific type of person, and then go find 10 of those people and ask them what they'd actually pay to solve it. Not what you'd charge, what they'd pay. That conversation, that uncomfortable, direct piece of market research, is where real digital product businesses start. Everything else is just production. Start with the problem.
