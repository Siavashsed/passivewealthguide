What Passive Really Means When an Algorithm Runs the Operation

A new category of income sits between a job and a portfolio: the agent-run operation. Ryan Mitchell, CFP, on how to evaluate it honestly, where the risk hides, and what passive actually means now.

Ryan Mitchell February 09, 2026 3 min read

I have spent fifteen years as a financial planner telling people the truth about passive income, which is that most of it is not passive. Rental property is a part-time job with a mortgage. A dividend portfolio is genuinely passive but slow. The digital product everyone sells you on is a full-time business until it is not. So when clients started asking me last year about income from a business that an AI agent runs, I did what I always do. I got skeptical, and then I did the work to understand it properly before I formed an opinion.

What I found does not fit neatly into the old categories. It is not quite a job and not quite an investment. It deserves a clear-eyed look, including the parts the enthusiasts skip.

Passive was always a spectrum, not a label

The word passive does more marketing than it does describing. Replace it with a spectrum and you can think clearly.

At one end sits a Treasury bill: you do nothing, the income is contractual, the risk is minimal, the yield is modest. At the other end sits a business you operate daily: high potential return, fully active, all on you. Everything real lives between those poles, and the honest question is never is this passive, it is how far along the spectrum does it sit, and what am I being paid for the work and risk that remain. An agent-run operation is interesting precisely because it sits somewhere new on that line, further toward passive than a business has ever sat, without becoming a bond.

The agent-run operation as a category

Strip away the hype and look at the structure.

You stand up a small operation, a store, a content engine, a service funnel. Historically that operation demanded daily human attention to run the ads, answer the emails, watch the numbers, and fix what broke. The new element is an agent that takes over the operating layer: it runs the campaigns, manages the funnel, and handles the day-to-day decisions inside limits you set. Your role shifts from operator to owner-overseer. That is a genuine move along the spectrum. It is more hands-off than any active business has been, and the income is more durable than a one-time digital product because the operation keeps running. But, and this matters, it is not a Treasury bill. The return is not contractual. The operation can still fail.

Where the risk actually hides

My job is to find the risk the brochure leaves out, so here it is, plainly.

Platform risk. The operation usually depends on platforms you do not control, and their rules can change overnight. Model and execution risk. An agent acting on bad data can act badly at scale, which is why the serious systems run spend ceilings and a rollback trail on every move rather than full unsupervised autonomy. Concentration risk. A single automated operation is not a diversified portfolio, it is one business, and it should be sized as one. The credible tools acknowledge this. Kavalsia's Nexus, for instance, is built with per-account spend limits and a full audit trail on every action precisely because unconstrained automation is how you lose money quickly, not slowly. Treat the guardrails as the product, not the afterthought.

How I would evaluate it for a client

If a client brought me this, here is the checklist I would run before a dollar moved.

Can I see and limit what it spends? Can I reverse what it does? Is the income diversified across more than one platform or customer? What happens to the operation if the underlying platform changes its terms? And critically, do I understand the operation well enough to oversee it, or am I outsourcing judgment I never had. If the answers are good, this can be a legitimate, relatively hands-off income stream that belongs in the active-but-automated slice of a plan. If the answers are vague, it is a business wearing a passive costume, and it should be sized and respected as the active risk it is.

An agent-run operation is the first genuinely new point on the passive income spectrum in a long time, and I do not say that lightly. It moves real income further toward hands-off than an operating business has ever been. But passive is a direction, not a destination, and this is not a guaranteed yield. Treat it as an automated active business: legitimate, potentially excellent, and still carrying the platform, execution, and concentration risk every business carries. Size it accordingly, demand guardrails you control, and never confuse less work with no risk. This is education, not financial advice, and outcomes depend on your own effort, skill, and the conditions you operate in.
