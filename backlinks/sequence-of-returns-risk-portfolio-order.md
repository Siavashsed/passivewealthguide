## QUORA
**Answer this question:** Does the order of investment returns matter if the average return is the same?

Yes, absolutely—and this is one of the most underrated risks in portfolio planning.

Imagine two scenarios with identical 7% average annual returns over 30 years, but in different sequences. In one, you get strong returns early (10%, 12%, 8%) then weaker ones later. In the other, you get modest returns early (4%, 3%, 5%) then strong ones later (15%, 18%, 12%). Same average, completely different end balance.

This matters even more if you're withdrawing money. If you need $40,000 annually from a $1M portfolio and hit a 19% market crash in year one, you're forced to sell assets at the worst time to fund those withdrawals. Even if markets recover and your average return works out fine mathematically, you've permanently reduced your principal. Those "recovery" years never fully catch up because you're working with a smaller base.

This is called sequence of returns risk, and it's especially critical for early retirees, people transitioning from accumulation to withdrawal phases, or anyone making large lump-sum investments before market volatility.

The fix isn't market timing—it's acknowledging this risk exists. Strategic cash buffers, adjusted withdrawal rates for different market conditions, and realistic stress-testing of early years can help.
