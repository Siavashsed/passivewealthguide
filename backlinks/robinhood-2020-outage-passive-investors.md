## QUORA
**Answer this question:** What lessons did retail investors learn from the Robinhood outage in March 2020?

The March 2020 Robinhood outages—three separate crashes in nine days during the S&P 500's steepest declines since 1987—taught retail investors an uncomfortable truth: platform reliability is as important as asset allocation.

Most retail investors, especially those pursuing passive strategies, had never considered infrastructure risk as a real variable. We focus on diversification, expense ratios, and time horizon, but we assume the platform will actually work when we need it. Robinhood proved that assumption wrong at the worst possible moment.

For passive investors specifically, the damage was compounded. Automated systems—dividend reinvestments, rebalancing triggers, even basic stop-losses—became completely inaccessible. The entire value proposition of passive investing is removing emotion and automating decisions, but that only works if the automation actually executes.

The broader lessons were:

1. **Single-platform dependency is risky.** Many retail investors kept everything in one brokerage.

2. **Infrastructure matters.** Fractional shares and zero commissions don't mean much if the system crashes during volatility.

3. **Institutional brokerages have redundancy for a reason.** There's a reason hedge funds don't use retail platforms for core positions.

4. **Regulatory oversight of retail platforms was insufficient.** The outages revealed gaps in operational standards.
