Yes, it's realistic-but it requires patience and the right expectations. I did it in about 14 years, retiring at 41, and I want to be honest about what that actually means.

First, the timeline depends on how much you can invest and what dividend yields you're targeting. I wasn't looking for high-risk, high-yield stocks. I focused on stable, established dividend payers with sustainable yields around 3-4%. That might sound modest, but compounding transforms modest returns over time.

The critical insight is this: you're not trying to retire and then withdraw from your portfolio. You're building to the point where dividend income covers your living expenses. That's fundamentally different from the 4% rule approach, and it's psychologically powerful because you're living off generated income, not principal.

What most people underestimate is the reinvestment phase. Every dividend I received went back into buying more shares. That accelerated the compounding exponentially. By year 10-12, the math started feeling almost effortless because the portfolio was generating enough dividends to buy significant positions without my own contributions.

The obstacles aren't mathematical-they're behavioral. Market volatility tests your conviction. The temptation to chase higher yields or try timing the market derails most people.
