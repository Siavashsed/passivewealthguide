Tax-Efficient Investing Strategies Most People Quietly Ignore

By Sarah Mills

Photo by Nataliya Vaitkevich / Pexels

Most people spend hours researching which ETF to buy and about four minutes thinking about where to hold it. That imbalance costs them real money. A Vanguard study from 2022 estimated that tax-efficient portfolio construction can add up to 1.5% in net annual returns for taxable investors. That's not a rounding error. Over 20 years, that gap compounds into something genuinely painful to look at on a spreadsheet.

My position is this: the order in which you do things in investing matters just as much as what you invest in. Tax efficiency isn't a bonus feature you optimize later. It's the structure you build first. And yet, the strategies I'm about to walk through are ones I almost never see regular investors using, even smart ones who read all the right books.

Asset Location Is the Strategy Nobody Actually Uses

Asset location means putting the right investments in the right type of account, not just the right investments in any account. The short version: bonds and REITs generate ordinary income, which gets taxed at your highest marginal rate. So they belong inside your tax-advantaged accounts, like a traditional IRA or 401(k). Index funds and growth-oriented ETFs, which are already fairly tax-efficient on their own, are better candidates for your taxable brokerage account.

When I was building my own passive income portfolio through my late thirties, I made the classic mistake early on. I held a high-yield bond fund in my taxable account for almost two years before I caught it. Moving it to my IRA and replacing it with a broad market ETF in the taxable account saved me a noticeable amount come tax time. It's the kind of thing your brokerage platform will never tell you to do because it doesn't serve their interests to prompt that conversation.

The misconception I hear most often is that this only matters for people in high tax brackets. That's not really true. Even someone in the 22% bracket is giving up meaningful returns by ignoring this. The math works at almost every income level.

Tax-Loss Harvesting Works, But Not the Way Most People Think

Tax-loss harvesting gets talked about a lot in financial media, usually in a way that makes it sound like a seasonal trick you do in December. The reality is it's a year-round discipline, and most people either don't do it at all or do it wrong. The idea is simple enough: sell a position that's down, realize the loss to offset capital gains elsewhere, then immediately buy a similar (but not identical) investment so you stay in the market.

The wash-sale rule is where people get tripped up. You can't sell a security at a loss and buy the same one back within 30 days, or the IRS disallows the loss. But you can sell a total market fund and buy a large-cap index fund that tracks something slightly different. I've seen clients do the harvest correctly but then panic and buy back the exact same ETF after 28 days. That small error wipes out the entire benefit.

One honest caveat: tax-loss harvesting defers taxes, it doesn't eliminate them. You're lowering your cost basis on the replacement investment, which means a bigger gain later. For most long-term passive investors this trade is still worth it, but it's not free money. Anyone selling you a strategy as though it's purely additive is oversimplifying.

The Roth Conversion Ladder People Leave on the Table

If you have money in a traditional IRA or 401(k) and you're in a lower-income year, converting a portion to a Roth account is one of the most underused strategies I've come across in nearly two decades of working with clients. You pay income tax on the converted amount now, at today's rate, and then that money grows and is withdrawn completely tax-free in retirement. For anyone expecting higher taxes in the future, that's a meaningful shift.

The ladder approach works like this: you convert a chunk each year, staying just below the next tax bracket threshold, and then let those converted funds sit for five years before touching them. After five years, they become accessible penalty-free. This strategy is what made early retirement financially realistic for me. I spent three years in my mid-thirties converting aggressively during leaner income years, which gave me a tax-free income stream in my forties that I didn't have to stress about.

The debate in financial planning circles is real, though. If tax rates drop significantly, or if you expect to be in a much lower bracket in retirement anyway, traditional contributions might actually serve you better. There's no universal answer. The right call depends on your income trajectory and your best guess about future policy, which no one can predict with confidence.

I-Bonds and Municipal Bonds Deserve More Attention Than They Get

Series I savings bonds are genuinely boring to talk about, which is probably why they're so underused. In 2022, when inflation hit hard, I-bond rates peaked at 9.62% annualized. People who had been quietly buying the annual $10,000 maximum per person for years were suddenly very popular at dinner parties. The interest is exempt from state and local taxes, and if you use the proceeds for qualified education expenses, it can be federal-tax-exempt too. The limits are real and the liquidity isn't great in the first year, but as a piece of a passive income puzzle, they earn their spot.

Municipal bonds tend to get dismissed as something only wealthy retirees use. But if you're in the 24% federal bracket or above and you're holding bonds in a taxable account, munis often produce a better after-tax yield than their corporate counterparts. The tax-equivalent yield formula is simple and takes about two minutes to calculate. In my work with clients over the years, I'd estimate fewer than one in ten actually ran the numbers before dismissing munis as irrelevant to them.

Neither of these is a magic solution. I-bonds have purchase limits. Munis carry credit risk that varies significantly by issuer. But as components of a broader tax-aware strategy, both tend to be ignored without a good reason, which is a different thing from ignoring them after careful consideration.

Pick one thing from this list and look at your own accounts today. Just one. Check where your bond holdings are sitting right now and whether they belong there. That single move, done once, can quietly shift your after-tax outcome for every year that follows. The people who build real passive income aren't usually doing something exotic. They're just doing the unglamorous, structural work that most investors keep meaning to get around to.
