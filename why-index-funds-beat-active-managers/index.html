Why Index Funds Beat 90% of Active Fund Managers (And What That Means for Your Money)

By Sarah Mills

Photo by RDNE Stock project / Pexels

Here's something that surprised me early in my planning career: some of the smartest, most hardworking people in finance, armed with Bloomberg terminals and six-figure research budgets, still can't beat a simple index fund over the long run. Not most of the time. Not even half the time. According to the S&P SPIVA report from 2023, around 92% of actively managed U.S. large-cap funds underperformed the S&P 500 over a 20-year period. That's not a bad year. That's two decades of trying and mostly failing to outpace a fund that just... holds everything.

I retired at 41, and index funds were the quiet engine behind that. Not the most exciting story, I know. There were no hot stock picks, no perfectly timed trades. Just consistent, low-cost market exposure compounding over years. My argument here is simple: for most people building long-term wealth, index funds aren't just a decent option, they're arguably the single best default strategy available. Let me show you why.

The Math That Active Managers Can't Escape

Before we talk about skill, let's talk about arithmetic. Every actively managed fund charges fees. Expense ratios for active funds typically run somewhere between 0.5% and 1.5% per year. A broad market index fund, by contrast, might charge 0.03% to 0.10%. That gap sounds small until you run the numbers over 30 years on a $100,000 portfolio. At 1% annual drag, you're giving up roughly $80,000 or more compared to the low-cost alternative. That's not a rounding error. That's a meaningful chunk of your retirement.

And it gets harder for active managers from there. Because they're all competing against each other, the market price already reflects the collective wisdom of thousands of professionals. When one manager buys a supposedly undervalued stock, another well-resourced manager is often on the other side of that trade. It's a zero-sum game before fees, and a losing game after them. This is what economists call the 'cost matters hypothesis,' and it's one of the most reliable concepts in personal finance.

So the managers who do beat the market in a given year mostly do so through luck or short-term concentration risk, not repeatable skill. And when you try to identify those managers in advance? The research is brutal. Past outperformance predicts future outperformance only slightly better than a coin flip, and often not even that.

What 'Beating the Market' Actually Requires

People assume active managers underperform because they're incompetent. That's actually a misconception. Many are brilliant. The problem is that markets are more efficient than we give them credit for, especially in large-cap U.S. equities where information travels instantly and analysts scrutinize every quarterly report within hours. Being smart isn't enough when everyone else in the room is equally smart and working with the same data.

To consistently beat the index, a manager needs to be right more often than the collective market, after fees, after taxes on turnover, and after the transaction costs of actively buying and selling. That's a very high bar. In my work with clients over the years, I'd occasionally meet someone who swore their actively managed fund was outperforming. And sometimes it was, for a stretch. But when we adjusted for the additional risk they were taking, or looked at a longer timeframe, the edge almost always evaporated.

But there are pockets where active management genuinely adds value. Small-cap emerging markets, certain alternative asset classes, and distressed debt situations can all reward skilled managers because those markets are less efficient. I'm not saying active management is always wrong. I'm saying it's wrong as a default for most investors building broad wealth over time. Nuance matters here.

The Tax Drag That Nobody Talks About Enough

Here's the part that rarely makes it into the glossy fund brochure. Active funds trade frequently. Every time a manager sells a winning position, that's a taxable event in a non-retirement account. Over a year, a fund with high portfolio turnover, sometimes 80% to 100% annually, can generate significant capital gains distributions that get passed directly to you, the shareholder. You owe tax on gains you didn't choose to realize, from trades you didn't make, sometimes in a year when the fund itself lost money overall.

Index funds, because they only trade when the underlying index changes, have dramatically lower turnover. Some broad index funds go years without triggering meaningful capital gains. When I ran my own numbers during my accumulation years, the tax efficiency of index funds added what I estimated to be somewhere between 0.3% and 0.6% in effective annual return on top of the fee savings. Compounded over 15 to 20 years, that's serious money.

This is especially worth thinking about if you're investing in taxable brokerage accounts alongside your retirement accounts. The tax-efficiency argument alone, separate from the performance argument, makes index funds worth a long look for anyone with money outside a 401(k) or IRA.

How to Actually Put This Into Practice Without Overcomplicating It

I've seen people get so deep in the weeds comparing index funds that they delay investing for months. Don't do that. The difference between a good index fund and a slightly better one is almost always smaller than the cost of waiting. For most people, a total market fund or an S&P 500 index fund from Vanguard, Fidelity, or Schwab is all they need as a core holding. Expense ratios at these providers now run as low as 0.00% to 0.03% for broad index products. That's as close to free as investing gets.

The practical setup I'd suggest is this: automate regular contributions into a low-cost index fund, reinvest dividends automatically, and review once a year to rebalance if your allocation has drifted significantly. That's the whole strategy. It's not flashy. But when I tested different approaches across my own portfolio and those of clients over roughly 15 years, the simple automated index approach beat more active strategies in the majority of cases, and it required far less time and emotional energy to maintain.

One thing to watch: don't confuse low cost with no thought required. You still need to choose an asset allocation that fits your timeline and risk tolerance. An index fund full of stocks will drop 30% to 40% in a bad year, and that's a real experience, not a theoretical one. The fund's simplicity doesn't protect you from your own panic selling. Having a plan for those moments, and sticking to it, is where the real work lives.

If you take one thing from this today, make it this: pull up whatever investment accounts you currently hold and check the expense ratios. Add them up. If you're paying more than 0.20% on average across your portfolio, that's a concrete place to start. Moving even a portion of your holdings into a low-cost index fund is a real, immediate action you can take this week. Not because it's trendy, but because the data, the math, and honestly, my own experience building wealth quietly over two decades, all point in the same direction.
