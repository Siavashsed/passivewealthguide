The High-Yield Savings Account Explosion of 2023: What Passive Income Investors Actually Gained

In 2023, HYSAs crossed 5% APY for the first time in 15 years. Here's what that meant for passive income investors-and what most people missed.

By   Siavash Sadighi Filed   Passive Income & Investing Read   6 min

By mid-2023, high-yield savings accounts were paying more than most dividend stocks. That's not a headline you'd have believed in 2020, when the best online savings rates were sitting at a pitiful 0.50%. I remember sitting at my desk in March 2023, looking at a Marcus by Goldman Sachs account I'd nearly closed the year before, and realizing it was now yielding 4.50% APY. I'd left about $18,000 parked in there almost by accident, and it had quietly earned more in the prior quarter than my first dividend stock did in its first full year. That stopped me cold. The question I kept coming back to: was this a real passive income opportunity, or just a short-term sugar rush from the Fed's rate hiking cycle?

The Federal Reserve raised rates 11 times between March 2022 and July 2023, bringing the federal funds rate to a 22-year high of 5.25% to 5.50%, according to the Federal Reserve's official Open Market Operations page. That created a window that most passive income investors hadn't seen in their adult lives. Online banks and credit unions rushed to compete. By August 2023, accounts at institutions like SoFi, Ally, and Marcus were advertising APYs between 4.50% and 5.25%. For anyone building a passive income portfolio, this wasn't background noise. It was a structural shift worth paying attention to.

Why 5% APY Was Such a Shock to the Passive Income World

For most of the 2010s, cash was essentially a penalty. Keeping money in a savings account meant watching inflation quietly eat your purchasing power while your dividend stocks and rental income did the real work. That mindset got baked into passive income strategy for over a decade. So when rates crossed 5% in 2023, a lot of investors genuinely didn't know how to categorize it. Was this income? Was it still just 'parking' money? The honest answer is that it became both.

The math changed fast. A $50,000 emergency fund sitting in a traditional bank account earning 0.01% APY generated about $5 per year. That same $50,000 in a 5% HYSA generated around $2,500 annually, with zero risk to principal (within FDIC limits of $250,000 per depositor per institution). For someone building passive income streams, that's not trivial. That's a meaningful contribution to monthly cash flow, and it required exactly zero active management.

I've seen this pattern repeat in my work with clients who'd been dismissing cash as 'dead money' for years. In early 2023, I started actively recommending HYSAs not as a replacement for equity-based income but as a complementary layer. The ones who listened added a reliable, low-volatility income stream to their portfolio at a moment when stock dividends were under pressure from rising borrowing costs. That combination mattered a lot more than most people recognized at the time.

The Numbers Behind the Boom and Who Actually Benefited

The HYSA surge wasn't just anecdotal. According to FDIC deposit flow data, American consumers moved an estimated $1 trillion out of traditional bank deposits and into higher-yielding alternatives between early 2022 and late 2023. A 2023 report from Bankrate found that the national average savings rate at traditional banks was still only 0.42% even as online banks were offering ten times that. The gap between what big legacy banks paid and what online competitors offered was arguably the widest it had ever been.

Who benefited most? People who were already financially organized. If you had an emergency fund, a down payment fund, or short-term cash reserves sitting somewhere intentional, 2023 rewarded you for that discipline in a way no recent year had. If your cash was scattered across checking accounts or forgotten savings accounts at big banks, most of it probably sat there earning almost nothing. The passive income win in 2023 went to people who were paying attention. For more on this, see How to Evaluate a High-Yield Savings Account in 2025 (Before the Rates Drop Again).

There's also a demographic angle worth mentioning. Retirees and near-retirees who'd been frustrated by bond yields and CD rates finally had a reasonable place to put the cash-equivalent portion of their portfolio. A 5% return on the conservative, low-risk slice of a retirement portfolio isn't spectacular, but it's real income. For someone with $200,000 in that bucket, that's $10,000 a year with no credit risk. Compare that to the broader story of what the HYSA boom really meant for passive income investors and you start to see how transformative this window actually was.

What Most People Got Wrong About HYSAs as a Passive Income Tool

Here's where I want to push back against some of the hype. A high-yield savings account is not a passive income strategy. It's a component of one. And in 2023, a lot of people made the mistake of treating it like a destination rather than a tool. I watched people pull money out of dividend-paying stocks or REITs to park it in a HYSA, reasoning that 5% guaranteed beats a 3.5% dividend yield with stock risk. That logic is seductive but incomplete.

The problem is duration. HYSA rates are variable. They follow the Fed. When the Fed eventually cuts rates (and as of late 2023, markets were already pricing in cuts in 2024), those juicy yields will compress. Dividend stocks, done well, tend to grow their payouts over time. A company that paid a 3.5% yield in 2023 might pay 4.5% on your original cost basis five years later. A HYSA won't do that. If you want to understand why the long-term compounding of dividends is so powerful compared to cash yield, I'd point you toward how dividend stocks build passive income over time, which covers exactly why chasing the higher short-term rate often costs you in the long run.

Okay, I'll be honest for a second. My friend Dave tried the HYSA arbitrage thing in mid-2023 where he moved literally everything into cash yielding 5.15% because he was convinced a recession was coming any week now. He called me pretty smug about it in July. By December he was quietly asking how to get back into dividend ETFs without feeling like an idiot. I'm not 100% sure the pure cash strategy works for anyone with a timeline longer than 12 to 18 months, and watching Dave's situation kind of confirmed that suspicion for me. Sometimes the most obvious-looking win isn't actually the right move for your whole portfolio.

How to Position HYSAs Within a Real Passive Income Stack

The right frame for a HYSA in 2023, and honestly still today, is as the cash layer of a multi-stream passive income portfolio. Not the foundation. Not the ceiling. The cash layer. Think of it as the part of your income portfolio that's doing three things simultaneously: earning a decent yield, staying liquid, and absorbing volatility from your higher-risk income streams like dividend stocks, rental income, or digital products.

In practice, that means keeping three to six months of expenses in a HYSA regardless of the rate environment, but in 2023 it also meant earmarking short-term capital that wasn't ready for longer-term deployment. Saving for a property down payment in the next 18 months? A 5% HYSA beats a bond fund with interest rate risk. Sitting on proceeds from a sold property while you scout your next investment? Same logic. This is the use case where HYSAs genuinely shine, and 2023 was the year a lot of investors finally understood that cash doesn't have to be lazy. For more on this, see The High-Yield Savings Boom of 2023: What It Really Meant for Passive Income Investors.

One thing worth building toward: if you're exploring other income streams alongside your HYSA, the learning curve for some of them is steeper than people expect. I've written before about how the first 18 months of digital product income rarely match expectations, and the cash buffer a well-funded HYSA provides can give you the runway to let those longer-horizon income streams develop without financial pressure. That combination, liquid cash earning real yield plus slower-building equity and product income, is what a sensible passive income stack looked like at the end of 2023.

The common misconception I keep correcting is that diversifying passive income means spreading money across dozens of accounts and products. It doesn't. It means spreading across income types: interest, dividends, rent, royalties. Each has a different relationship with inflation, interest rates, and your own time. A HYSA handles the interest-rate-sensitive, liquid piece of that stack. In 2023, that piece finally started earning its keep.

If you haven't already, open a high-yield savings account this week and move your emergency fund and any cash reserves sitting in a traditional bank into it. Rates will eventually fall, but right now you're likely leaving hundreds or thousands of dollars on the table every year by keeping cash at 0.01% APY when 4% to 5% is available with zero additional risk. That's the most straightforward, no-excuses passive income win available to almost anyone in 2023, and it takes about 20 minutes to set up.
