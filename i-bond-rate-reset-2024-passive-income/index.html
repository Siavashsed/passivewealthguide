Treasury Direct Hit 9.62%: What the I-Bond Frenzy of 2024 Actually Taught Us

I-Bonds dominated passive income talk in 2024. Ryan Mitchell breaks down what the rate reset meant, who got burned, and what to do now.

Lucia Mendes August 05, 2024 5 min read

The I-Bond party that millions of Americans crashed in 2022 and 2023 hit its hangover moment in 2024, and a lot of people weren't ready for it. I bought $10,000 in I-Bonds in October 2022, sitting at my kitchen table at 11pm refreshing TreasuryDirect.gov like it was a concert ticket drop. The rate was 9.62% annualized. I felt like I'd found a cheat code. Then May 2024 arrived, the composite rate dropped to 4.28%, and I had to actually think about whether these things still made sense in my portfolio.

Here's the thing about I-Bonds that most of the enthusiasm in 2022 and 2023 quietly glossed over: the rate resets every six months based on the CPI-U, and the fixed rate component, the part that stays with your bond for its 30-year life, was 0.0% for bonds issued before November 2022. So when inflation cooled, holders of those early bonds were left with a variable rate tied to a CPI that was heading back toward normal. The 2024 rate reset forced a genuine reckoning with what passive income from government savings bonds actually looks like when the emergency conditions that made them famous disappear.

How the 2024 Rate Reset Played Out, Month by Month

The TreasuryDirect official rate history tells a pretty clear story. The May 2024 composite rate landed at 4.28% for new purchases, down from 5.27% in November 2023. For bondholders sitting on the 0.0% fixed rate from 2022, that composite rate was purely inflation-driven. Compare that to I-Bonds issued in November 2022 onward, which carried a 0.40% fixed rate, and bonds from May 2024, which locked in a 1.30% fixed rate. That fixed rate difference is not cosmetic. Over a 20-year hold, a 1.30% fixed component adds up to thousands of dollars in real purchasing power above inflation, compounded annually.

The investor community noticed. Reddit's r/personalfinance, which had threads with tens of thousands of upvotes celebrating I-Bonds in 2022, shifted tone noticeably by mid-2024. The question stopped being "how do I buy more" and became "should I redeem and move to a high-yield savings account or short-term Treasuries." For people past their 12-month lockup and beyond the 5-year penalty-free window, the math genuinely favored looking elsewhere, at least in the short term. For those still inside the one-year lockup, they had no choice but to wait and watch.

According to The Wall Street Journal, net I-Bond sales turned negative in early 2024 for the first time since the pandemic-era buying surge, meaning more people were redeeming than buying. That's not a crash. But it's a signal that the asset class had moved from "obvious" back to "situational."

The Real Mistake Most I-Bond Buyers Made (And I Made It Too)

I'll be honest about my own error here. When I bought in October 2022, I bought the full $10,000 individual limit and didn't think hard about the fixed rate. I was chasing the headline number. The 9.62% felt real and urgent and the fixed component of 0.0% felt like fine print. It wasn't fine print. It was the most important line on the page. For more on this, see I Bond Rates Hit 9.62%: What That Number Actually Changed in 2022.

My friend tried this with her husband's account too, maxing both their individual limits and their trust account, putting in $30,000 total at the 0.0% fixed rate. Honestly, she told me in March 2024 that she felt a little silly, because by then a 13-week Treasury bill was yielding around 5.3% with no lockup at all. I'm not 100% sure the I-Bond decision was wrong for her in the long run, because she's holding them past year five and treating them like inflation insurance, not a savings account. But the opportunity cost stung. That's a very real trade-off that the enthusiastic blog posts from 2022 mostly skipped.

The misconception worth naming: I-Bonds are not a high-yield savings account. They're a long-duration inflation hedge with a one-year minimum hold and a liquidity penalty in years two through five. When you treat them like a savings account, the 2024 rate reset feels like a betrayal. When you treat them like what they actually are, a 30-year government instrument that guarantees your purchasing power, the reset is just... how the thing works.

What the 2024 Reset Meant for Passive Income Strategy More Broadly

The I-Bond story in 2024 isn't really about I-Bonds. It's about what happens when a passive income tool gets overhyped during an anomalous rate environment and then has to prove itself in normal conditions. We saw the same pattern with high-yield savings accounts, with short-term CD ladders, and to some extent with money market funds. The Fed's rate moves in 2024 shaped all of it. If you want context on how those Fed decisions rippled through passive income strategies more broadly, I wrote about it here: how the Fed's rate pivot in 2024 actually affected passive income investors.

Federal Reserve data shows the effective federal funds rate peaked above 5.3% in mid-2023 and began moving lower through 2024. That shift compressed yields on cash-equivalent instruments across the board, I-Bonds included. Investors who had parked money in "safe" rate-sensitive products found themselves needing to think more carefully about where yield was actually going to come from over the next three to five years.

The investors who came through 2024 best, in my view, were the ones who used the high-rate window to build a more permanent income structure rather than just stacking rate-sensitive products. Dividend-paying equities, REITs with strong balance sheets, and even some digital income streams proved more durable. If you're thinking about the dividend side of that picture, this piece on how dividend stocks build passive income over time lays out the compounding logic that made a real difference in my own portfolio through the rate chop of 2024.

Who Should Still Own I-Bonds After the 2024 Reset

The case for I-Bonds in 2024 and beyond is narrower than it was in 2022, but it's not gone. Bonds purchased from May 2024 onward carry a 1.30% fixed rate, which is the highest fixed component since 2007, according to TreasuryDirect's own rate table. Over a 20 or 30 year holding period, that fixed rate sitting on top of CPI-U inflation is genuinely attractive, especially for someone who wants to ring-fence a portion of their savings against inflation without taking equity risk. For more on this, see The I Bond Frenzy of 2022: What It Really Meant for Passive Income Investors.

The people who make the most sense as I-Bond buyers right now are in their 40s or 50s, building toward retirement, and want a government-backed inflation floor on a portion of their fixed income allocation. Not a replacement for dividend stocks or REITs. A complement. The people who probably shouldn't buy them are anyone who might need the money within 12 months, or anyone treating them as a substitute for a proper emergency fund. Liquidity matters, and I-Bonds don't have it in the short term.

  • Best use case: Long-hold inflation hedge, 10 to 30 year horizon, used alongside equities.
  • Poor use case: Short-term cash parking, especially if you're inside the one-year lockup window.
  • Watch out for: The annual $10,000 purchase limit per person (plus $5,000 in paper bonds via tax refund) makes this a modest position for most portfolios, not a core strategy on its own.

And here's the thing... the fixed rate of 1.30% won't last forever. TreasuryDirect resets it every November and May. So if you've been sitting on the fence about whether current I-Bonds are worth it, the window on that 1.30% fixed rate is finite. Watching it is worth your time even if acting on it isn't urgent today.

If you bought I-Bonds at the 0.0% fixed rate in 2022, log into TreasuryDirect today and check your composite rate for the current six-month period. If you're past year five and your composite rate is running below what a 6-month Treasury bill is paying right now, run the math on redeeming and rebuying at the 1.30% fixed rate. You'll reset the clock on the one-year lockup, but you'll also lock in inflation protection that's meaningfully better over any hold period longer than five years. That specific calculation is the one most people aren't doing, and it's where the real money is hiding in this story.

Frequently asked questions

Is it worth buying I-Bonds in 2024 with the lower rate?

Bonds issued from May 2024 carry a 1.30% fixed rate, the highest since 2007, which makes them worth considering for long-hold inflation protection. If you plan to hold for 10 or more years, that fixed component stacks meaningfully on top of whatever CPI does. For short-term needs, a high-yield savings account or short-term Treasury bill is more flexible.

Should I redeem my 2022 I-Bonds now that rates have dropped?

If you're past the five-year mark, there's no early redemption penalty, and it's worth comparing your current composite rate against short-term Treasuries or a new I-Bond with a 1.30% fixed rate. Before year five, you forfeit three months of interest on redemption, so the math depends on how far in you are and what rates are doing at that moment.

What is the I-Bond purchase limit per year?

Each person can buy $10,000 in electronic I-Bonds per calendar year through TreasuryDirect, plus an additional $5,000 in paper bonds using a federal tax refund. Married couples can double the electronic limit by buying separately, and a trust or business entity can add another $10,000 on top of that.
