The Fed Just Cut Rates to Zero. Your Savings Account Is Now a Rounding Error

In March 2020 the Fed slashed rates to near zero and gutted savings yields. Here's what it means for passive income investors right now.

Ryan Mitchell December 30, 2020 4 min read

On Sunday night, March 15, the Federal Reserve did something it usually saves for a full-blown crisis: it cut its benchmark rate to a range of 0 to 0.25 percent in a single move, weeks before its scheduled meeting. If you own a high-yield savings account, that number you were proud of is about to melt. I logged into my Ally account Monday morning, coffee in hand, and the rate had already dropped from 1.60 percent to 1.50, with a note that more cuts were coming. By the time you read this, it might start with a zero.

So here's the honest version. The safe, boring corner of passive income, the part built on savings accounts, CDs, and short-term Treasuries, just got a lot less rewarding. And it happened almost overnight. For anyone who spent the last few years parking cash and collecting a modest but real yield, this is a genuine reset, and I think most people haven't felt the full weight of it yet.

What the Fed actually did, in plain terms

The Fed controls a short-term rate that ripples out to almost everything else. When they drop it to near zero, banks stop competing for your deposits, because they can borrow cheaply elsewhere. So the interest they pay you falls fast.

According to the Federal Reserve's own March 15 statement, this was an emergency response to the economic hit from the coronavirus. They also announced $700 billion in bond buying. That last part matters, because it pushes down yields on Treasuries and bonds too, the other place conservative income investors hide.

The upshot is blunt. Almost every low-risk income source you know just took a pay cut at the same time. This is not a dip you wait out over a few weeks. Rates like this tend to stay low for a long stretch once the Fed commits, and they've signaled they're committed.

Why the 'safe yield' playbook is breaking

For years the advice was simple. Keep your emergency fund and short-term cash in a high-yield savings account earning 1.5 to 2 percent, and call it passive income. It wasn't exciting, but it was real money for doing nothing. For more on this, see How to Evaluate a High-Yield Savings Account in 2025 (Before the Rates Drop Again).

That math is now gutted. On $30,000 in savings, dropping from 1.6 percent to, say, 0.5 percent is the difference between roughly $480 a year and $150. Same money, same risk, a third of the reward. I've watched this pattern before, back in the 2010s when rates sat near zero for most of the decade, and the frustrating part is how quietly it erodes. You don't notice month to month. You notice a year later when your interest is a fraction of what it was.

If you want the full story of how these accounts behave through a rate cycle, I wrote about it later in what the high-yield savings boom really meant for passive income investors. The short version: these accounts are wonderful when rates are high and nearly pointless when they're not. Right now, they're not.

Where income investors are turning next

When cash stops paying, money goes looking. And in weeks like this, it usually flows toward a few places. Some are reasonable. Some will burn people.

Here's what I'm seeing and where I'd be careful:

  • Dividend stocks. Solid companies paying 3 to 4 percent suddenly look attractive next to a 0.5 percent savings account. But stock prices are swinging 5 to 9 percent a day right now, so this is not a cash substitute. It's a different risk entirely.
  • Dividend cuts are already starting. A yield only counts if the company keeps paying it. Several firms in travel and retail are signaling they may suspend dividends to preserve cash during the shutdown.
  • Reaching for junk. The dangerous move is chasing 8 percent yields in shaky companies or exotic funds just to replace lost interest. That's how people turn a safe pile of cash into a real loss.

If you're thinking about dividends as a longer-term income base rather than a cash parking spot, I laid out how I actually use them in how dividend stocks build passive income over time. The core idea holds even now: buy for the durability of the payment, not the size of the yield.

The uncomfortable case for income you control

Here's my actual opinion, and it's a little contrarian for a passive income writer. Weeks like this are a loud argument for income sources that don't depend on a central bank's mood.

My friend Dave tried to build his whole retirement income around laddered CDs a few years back, and honestly, watching him now is a little painful. He did everything the safe way, and the Fed just quietly repriced his plan without asking. I'm not sure the pure cash-and-CD approach ever really works for the long haul, because you're renting a rate that someone else can cancel on a Sunday night. That's not passive. That's just waiting. For more on this, see The High-Yield Savings Account Explosion of 2023: What Passive Income Investors Actually Gained.

So I keep coming back to income you build and own. A small digital product, a piece of content that keeps earning, a skill that pays. It's more work up front, and it's slower than clicking 'open savings account.' I've spent years on this, and the first year and a half is genuinely rough, which I got into in what nobody tells you about the first 18 months of digital products. But nobody can cut that rate to zero over the weekend.

Do one thing this week: pull up your savings and CD accounts and write down the actual dollar interest you earned last month. Not the percentage, the dollars. That number is your real starting point, and it's about to shrink. Once you see it in plain money, you'll make far better decisions about whether chasing yield, buying quality dividend payers, or building your own income stream is worth the effort for you.

Frequently asked questions

Will my high-yield savings account rate keep dropping after this?

Almost certainly, yes. Once the Fed cuts to near zero, banks lower deposit rates within days to weeks. Expect your rate to keep sliding until it settles well under 1 percent, and it can stay there for a year or more.

Are dividend stocks a safe replacement for my savings interest?

No, they're a different risk level entirely. Stock prices can drop 20 to 30 percent in weeks, and companies can suspend dividends during a downturn. Treat dividends as long-term income, not a swap for cash you might need soon.

Should I lock money into a CD before rates fall further?

A short-term CD can lock a slightly better rate today, but you're still renting a low yield and tying up cash. Keep your emergency fund liquid first, and only ladder CDs with money you truly won't touch.
