Royalty Investments: How to Earn Passive Income from Music, Books, and Patents

Music Royalties: The Most Accessible Entry Point for Most Investors

Music royalties come in a few flavors. Performance royalties are paid when a song is played on radio, streamed, or performed live. Mechanical royalties come from physical and digital reproductions. Sync royalties kick in when music gets licensed for TV, film, or advertising. As an investor, you're typically buying a share of one or more of these revenue streams from an existing catalog. Platforms like Royalty Exchange and ANote Music have made this possible with minimums starting around $100 to $500 in some cases.

Billboard has reported that the global recorded music market grew to over $28 billion in 2023, which tells you there's real money flowing through these pipes. The question is how much of it flows to small investors. In my work with clients who've added music royalties to their portfolios, the realistic yield tends to land somewhere between 5% and 15% annually, though that range hides a lot of variance. A catalog tied to a song that goes viral or lands a major sync deal can spike dramatically. One tied to a forgotten artist from the 1990s might slowly decline as streams drop off.

The key risk here is catalog decay. Songs get played less over time, in most cases. So you're not buying a perpetual annuity - you're buying a declining cash flow stream, which means the price you pay at entry matters enormously. Always look at the trailing 12-month revenue, not the peak year, before bidding on anything. For more on this, see How Dividend Stocks Build Passive Income Over Time (And Why I Trusted Them With My Retirement).

Book and Literary Royalties: Slower, But Often More Predictable

Book royalties work differently than music. Authors earn a percentage of each sale - typically 10% to 15% of the cover price for traditionally published print books, and up to 70% on some self-published digital titles through platforms like Amazon KDP. Investors don't often buy individual book royalties the way they do with music, but there are indirect routes worth knowing about.

Some authors sell future royalty rights to investors directly, essentially in exchange for upfront capital. Think of it as a royalty advance from a private source rather than a publisher. Publishers Weekly has covered the rise of revenue-based financing for authors, where investors fund a book's production costs in exchange for a share of royalties until a return threshold is met. I'm not 100% sure this works for everyone, but I watched a friend of mine - Marcus, a financial writer in Austin - put $8,000 into a deal like this with a personal finance author whose backlist was already selling steadily. He got his money back in about 26 months and walked away with roughly a 19% total return. He also spent six months mildly stressed about it because the author released the sequel two months late and sales dipped. So, you know, read the contract really carefully. There's a reason Marcus now says the return wasn't worth the cortisol.

The more passive version is investing in a company that holds a large portfolio of literary IP. Some REITs and closed-end funds hold intellectual property assets, though pure-play literary royalty funds are rare. For most individual investors, the direct author deal or a platform like Royalty Exchange remains the most practical route.

Patent Royalties: Higher Yield, Higher Complexity

Patent royalties are where things get genuinely interesting, and genuinely complicated. A patent grants its holder the exclusive right to commercialize an invention for a set period (usually 20 years from the filing date in the US). Companies that don't want to manufacture products themselves sometimes license those patents to others in exchange for royalties. Investors can buy into those royalty streams through patent holding companies, pharmaceutical royalty funds, or direct private deals. For more on this, see How to Build a $1,000/Month Passive Income Stream From Scratch (A Real Plan, Not a Fantasy).

Pharmaceutical patent royalties are the most established corner of this market. According to Royalty Pharma, the largest buyer of biopharmaceutical royalties globally, the royalty market for approved drugs alone has been valued in the hundreds of billions of dollars. Royalty Pharma itself is publicly traded (ticker: RPRX), which gives ordinary investors a clean, liquid way to hold a diversified portfolio of drug royalties without negotiating private deals. Their dividend yield has hovered around 2% to 3%, but total return including capital appreciation has been meaningful for long-term holders.

Outside pharma, technology patent royalties exist but are harder to access as a passive investor. Most deals happen in private markets, require significant capital, and demand legal due diligence that frankly costs more than most retail investors can justify. If you're starting out, Royalty Pharma or a fund with patent exposure is a much smarter starting point than trying to buy a tech patent outright. If you want to see how royalty assets fit into a broader passive income plan, the framework I laid out in building a $1,000/month passive income stream from scratch gives you a way to think about sizing these positions relative to your other income sources.

What Royalty Investing Actually Requires From You

Here's where I push back against the 'set it and forget it' narrative that surrounds this asset class. Royalty investments are more passive than running a business, sure. But they're less passive than, say, owning an index fund. You need to understand what you're buying, monitor cash flows, and know when a stream is declining structurally versus just having a bad quarter.

The due diligence list for any royalty purchase should include at minimum: the trailing 24 months of revenue, the source and diversity of payers, the term remaining on any underlying contracts, and what happens if the primary payer defaults or goes out of business. That last point gets glossed over constantly. The SEC's guidance on alternative investments is worth reading before you put money into any private royalty deal - it won't tell you which deals to choose, but it'll sharpen your sense of what questions to ask. For more on this, see Passive Income With Digital Products: What Nobody Tells You About the First 18 Months.

I've seen this pattern play out more times than I'd like: someone buys a royalty stream that looks great on a one-year trailing basis, doesn't notice that revenue is down 30% from two years prior, and ends up stuck in a declining asset they paid a premium for. The fix is simple but requires discipline. Always ask for at least three years of revenue data. And compare your expected yield to what you could earn from dividend stocks that build passive income over time, because dividends from quality companies offer more liquidity and, in many cases, similar or better risk-adjusted returns. Royalties should complement that kind of foundation, not replace it.

  • Music royalties: Most accessible, platform-driven, yields vary widely, watch for catalog decay
  • Book royalties: Less liquid, often structured as private deals, better for patient capital
  • Patent royalties: Highest complexity, pharma royalty funds offer the most practical public entry point
  • All royalties: Require real due diligence on historical revenue trends before committing capital
